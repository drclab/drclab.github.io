Welcome to JAX 305. This post is based on Section 5 (Evaluation) of *Probabilistic Programming with Vectorized Programmable Inference*.

After JAX 301-304 built up motivation, formalism, and implementation, Section 5 asks the practical question: **does this design actually deliver speed and inference quality in hard problems?**

## 1. Evaluation goals in Section 5

The paper evaluates two dimensions:

1. **Performance**: overhead and scaling relative to handcoded and popular open-source PPL/tensor frameworks.
2. **Inference quality**: whether vectorized programmable inference can solve high-dimensional problems accurately.

The comparison set includes handcoded JAX and frameworks such as NumPyro, Pyro, and Gen.jl in representative tasks.

## 2. Performance survey: key takeaway

### 2.1 Beta-Bernoulli importance sampling

A simple but revealing benchmark compares posterior inference quality and runtime for a coin-bias model.

Result: GenJAX is effectively at handcoded JAX speed in this setup (roughly ~100% relative runtime), while preserving high-level programmable abstractions.

Interpretation: abstraction overhead is small enough to be practically negligible in this task.

### 2.2 Polynomial regression with importance sampling and HMC

Section 5 then compares scaling behavior on a richer model.

- **Importance sampling**: near-constant runtime over particle growth while GPU resources are not saturated.
- **HMC**: runtime scales with chain length (as expected for iterative dynamics).

Result: GenJAX remains consistently close to handcoded JAX across these modes, with substantial speedups relative to less-optimized paths in some settings.

## 3. High-dimensional case study I: Game of Life inversion

The first large-scale demonstration is probabilistic inversion of Conway’s Game of Life.

Problem setup:

- Observe a future board state.
- Infer a plausible previous state.
- Naive brute-force inversion is combinatorially intractable.

Section 5 strategy:

1. Build a probabilistic forward model (deterministic evolution + observation noise).
2. Use vectorized Gibbs sampling for approximate inversion.
3. Exploit conditional independence structure to update cell groups in parallel.

Reported outcome:

- High reconstruction quality (around 90%+ in the reported large-board experiment).
- End-to-end runtimes in seconds on modern GPU for large grids.

Takeaway: vectorization is not just for toy models; it enables practical inference in massive discrete latent spaces.

## 4. High-dimensional case study II: robot localization

The second case study addresses 2D localization with simulated LIDAR observations.

Inference programs compared include:

- Bootstrap particle filter (SMC with prior proposal).
- SMC plus HMC rejuvenation moves.
- SMC with a locally optimal proposal built from vectorized likelihood-grid approximations.

Section 5 emphasizes that vectorization appears at multiple levels:

1. Vectorized observation model (LIDAR rays).
2. Vectorized proposal-grid evaluation.
3. Vectorization over particle populations.

Result: the locally optimal SMC variant achieves strong efficiency-accuracy tradeoffs, with fast tracking and improved posterior quality.

## 5. What this means for JAX users

Section 5 supports a practical engineering claim:

- Keep inference programmable,
- expose vectorization opportunities explicitly,
- and compose algorithms around reusable interfaces.

When done well, you can get near-handcoded performance while retaining flexibility to iterate on proposal design and hybrid kernels.

## 6. A reusable recipe from Section 5

For your own projects, the evaluation suggests this sequence:

1. Start with a baseline vectorized algorithm (e.g., IS or bootstrap SMC).
2. Measure scaling against particle count / chain length.
3. Add structure-aware proposals or rejuvenation steps (e.g., Gibbs, HMC).
4. Vectorize every independent axis you can identify.
5. Compare quality and runtime jointly, not in isolation.

## 7. Summary

Section 5 validates the full stack introduced in earlier sections:

- performance remains close to hand-optimized JAX,
- abstraction cost is low,
- and programmable vectorized inference extends to genuinely difficult high-dimensional tasks.

This is the empirical confirmation that the formal and compiler ideas are not only correct, but useful.
